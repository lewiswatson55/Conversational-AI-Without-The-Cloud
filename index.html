📝 Abstract

A major limitation of current social robots is their dependence on cloud-based dialogue pipelines, which restricts use in settings with limited or unreliable connectivity. We present a lightweight, fully local spoken-dialogue system that runs on consumer-grade hardware and integrates open-source models for speech recognition, dialogue generation, and text-to-speech. The pipeline was deployed on Euclid, a non-commercial humanoid robot, across several public engagement events, enabling extended real-world interaction without internet access. We analyse over 5,000 dialogue turns recorded during these dialogues to characterise system behaviour, user interaction patterns, and challenges arising in noisy, multi-speaker environments. Our observations demonstrate the feasibility of privacy-preserving, on-device conversational robotics while highlighting limitations in turn-taking, response length, and environmental grounding.

💡 Key Findings

  • A fully local dialogue pipeline can run reliably on a single consumer-grade GPU laptop for hours of uninterrupted public interaction without internet access.
  • Interactions were overwhelmingly positive — 79% of user turns and 75% of Euclid's turns were classified as positive sentiment by a DistilBERT classifier.
  • Users tended to produce short, greeting-oriented turns, while the assistant generated longer, content-rich responses — sometimes leading to excessive verbosity that disrupted conversational pacing.
  • Noisy multi-party environments caused significant turn-taking breakdowns: VAD misinterpreted natural pauses as turn-endings, triggering premature responses.
  • Topic modelling (BERTopic) revealed Euclid's responses consistently reflected its "old robot with rusty circuits" persona, validating the dynamic prompt controller design.
  • The modular architecture enables model swaps (e.g., Qwen3 4B tested as a drop-in replacement) without modifying the dialogue manager.

🔭 Future Work

  • Robust turn-taking: interruption (barge-in) detection and optional push-to-talk modes.
  • Personality distillation: fine-tuning concise, characterful responses using collected dialogue data and manually authored examples.
  • Visual grounding: lightweight presence or gaze detection to anchor responses to the immediate environment.
  • Formal user studies: HRI questionnaires and controlled local-vs-cloud comparisons to quantify effects on trust, warmth, and perceived intelligence.
